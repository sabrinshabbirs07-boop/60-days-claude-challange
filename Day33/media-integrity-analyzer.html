Concept · Headline Accuracy

Headlines are written to be clicked, not to be complete.

A headline's job is to earn a click in under two seconds — often written by an editor who never wrote the article. That pressure can pull a headline away from what the story actually supports.

The gap between what a headline implies and what the article proves is called the accuracy gap. The wider the gap, the more likely you're being sold an impression rather than informed.

Why it matters: most people only read the headline. If it overstates the story, that overstatement is often the only "fact" that sticks.

Concept · Emotional Manipulation

Feelings travel faster than facts.

Content that makes you feel something — outrage, fear, awe, envy — gets shared more than content that simply informs. That's not an accident; word choice, pacing, and framing are often tuned specifically to trigger a reaction.

This doesn't always mean something is false. It means the delivery is designed to bypass your slower, more critical thinking.

Why it matters: the stronger the emotional pull, the more worth pausing before you share, buy, or believe.
